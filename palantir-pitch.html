Palantir Technologies (NASDAQ: PLTR)

A SHORT recommendation. Palantir is a genuinely strong business priced for a near-impossible outcome — the market is paying a software premium for revenue that is, at its core, lumpy, government-weighted and concentrated. We see the multiple, not the company, as the trade.

Even if Palantir doubled its profits every year for the next three years, at today's price it would still trade on a P/E of roughly 75×. The bar the price implies is not ambitious — it is close to unrepeatable.

01A valuation disconnected from fundamentals

Thesis · Multiple compression

Palantir is a high-quality compounder — FY24 revenue of ~$2.87bn grew 29%, gross margins sit near 80%, and the business is now consistently profitable. None of that is in dispute. The dispute is price.

On next-twelve-month multiples Palantir trades at an extreme premium to every comparable in the software and AI universe:

  • EV/Revenue ~81× versus a peer set (Microsoft, ServiceNow, CrowdStrike, Salesforce, Oracle, Adobe) clustered between roughly 5× and 30×.
  • EV/EBITDA ~172× and P/E ~231× — multiples of the next-highest names, not a modest premium to them.
  • A forward P/E near 375×, against ~53× for Nvidia and ~36× for Microsoft.

A premium this large can only be justified by a growth path that compounds at exceptional rates for years without stumbling. Historical revenue CAGR has run around 27–30%; to grow into the current valuation, the implied required CAGR is closer to 80–100% over the medium term. Any quarter of guidance deceleration is therefore likely to trigger sharp multiple compression — the asymmetry that makes this a short.

02Profitability is flattered, disclosure is thin

Thesis · Earnings quality

Reported profitability leans heavily on stock-based compensation, which runs at roughly 16% of revenue. Strip that out and "adjusted" earnings look materially less impressive, which matters a great deal when the valuation rests almost entirely on the trajectory of those earnings.

Compounding this, Palantir provides limited standalone disclosure on AIP — the AI platform doing much of the work in the bull narrative. The premium multiple is effectively underwritten by performance the market cannot directly verify, increasing the risk of a re-rating once the numbers are tested against expectations.

03A structural ceiling on government growth

Thesis · Concentration & procurement risk

Government work — defence, intelligence and national security — accounts for roughly 55% of revenue. That base is sticky, but it is also capped: government contracts carry termination rights, procurement is slow and highly competitive, and there is a finite volume of high-value awards the US government can route to a single vendor.

  • Budget sensitivity: the share price has historically reacted disproportionately to defence-spending headlines, underlining how exposed the story is to political and fiscal cycles.
  • Execution risk: the troubled NHS Federated Data Platform programme illustrates the friction Palantir faces scaling overseas.
  • Emerging competition: Govini — founded by a former Palantir national-security executive — has scaled past $100m ARR and won a ~$919m IDIQ for its Ark platform. A credible, vertical AI competitor inside Palantir's strongest franchise would force investors to reprice its long-term federal growth.

The investment case also assumes government project work converts into a scalable, SaaS-like platform. We think the market overestimates how cleanly that translation happens.

Valuation

DCF · base case & sensitivity

Our discounted-cash-flow model points to a base-case fair value of $74.12 — about 58% below the current price. The base case uses a WACC of 15.8% and a 5% terminal growth rate, with a cross-check against an exit-multiple approach (~12× revenue) that implies a similar direction of travel.

The conclusion is robust to reasonable changes in assumptions. Flexing WACC across 14.8%–16.8% and terminal growth across 3%–7% keeps the implied downside firmly negative across the entire grid:

Catalysts

What forces the re-rating
  • Guidance deceleration. With the price implying near-flawless growth, any softening in forward guidance is the most direct trigger for multiple compression.
  • FY2027 DoD budget (Jan–Mar 2026). The first formal venue where competitor Govini's momentum could appear in hard budget allocations — recognition of a credible federal-AI rival would pressure Palantir's long-term growth narrative.
  • AIP scrutiny. As investors push for clearer AIP economics, thinner-than-hoped disclosure could undermine the premium directly.

Risks to the thesis

What would prove us wrong

A short on a high-momentum, narrative-driven stock carries real risk, and intellectual honesty means naming it:

  • AIP adoption accelerates. Faster commercial uptake (US commercial revenue rose sharply through 2025) could validate the growth path we doubt.
  • Major contract wins. Reported large UK MoD and UK investment deals, plus rising global defence budgets (SIPRI noted defence spending up ~9%), could extend the runway.
  • Sticky retail base. A meme-like holder base has driven shares up ~130% YTD despite few "Buy" ratings; sentiment can stay irrational longer than a short can stay comfortable — hence the 2–3 year horizon and position sizing.

We size and time the trade accordingly: the thesis is a valuation re-rating over years, not a call on the next print.
